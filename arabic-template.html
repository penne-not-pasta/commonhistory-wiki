The Black Death

The Black Death was a bubonic plague pandemic, occurring in Western Eurasia and North Africa from 1347 to 1351. It is the most fatal pandemic recorded in human history, causing the death of 75–200 million people.

Historical Context

The plague was caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, spread by fleas. The consequences of this event reshaped the medieval world, leading to drastic changes in religion, art, and the feudal economy.

"Father abandoned child, wife husband, one brother another; for this illness seemed to strike through the breath and sight." — Agnolo di Tura, 1348

Impact on Society

Labor shortages led to higher wages for peasants, effectively signaling the beginning of the end for the traditional manorial system.
